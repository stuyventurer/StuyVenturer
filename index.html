Learning About the NBA

I started keeping up with the NBA when I was in the beginning fifth grade (around mid to late 2016) because I saw that my friends were interested in it.
At first, I was slightly curious, but overtime, I became more curious, and since then, I have always been always trying to learn about random facts about the NBA.

I've had many phases when it comes to learning about the NBA:

Checking the NBA Standings

I began learning about the NBA by checking the standings everyday when I woke up. I would at the box scores of all the games from the previous night, and I would try to identify the teams that went up and went down in the standings. I don't remember anything else besides the fact that the Knicks were 1st seed around 10-20 games into the 2016-2017 season and then dropped to the bottom of the standings by the end of the season. It was when Carmelo Anthony was still considered an all-star and when Kristaps Porzingis was showing flashes of what he would become the following season.

Googling NBA Players

I started learning about particular NBA players by searching up terms such as "NBA players" or "Knicks Players" or "Celtics Players." Whenever I did that, a list of NBA players would come up, and I would click on each of the NBA players photos and then look at their featured Wikipedia profile on the right. This gave me a very general view of who they were, as I would find out how they looked like, when they played/retired, how tall they were (which gives me an idea of what position they played), when they were drafted, and what pick they were drafted at. This sometimes took at least 40 minutes, and when you do this many times, you end up recognizing a lot of NBA players past and present.

Watching NBA highlights for the games from the previous day

Afterwards, I started going on YouTube and watching the game highlights/recaps from the previous night. My parents don't pay for any TV channels, so I don't get to watch any actual games unless I go to websites that illegally stream them (which I don't do). I have watched many channels, but some notable ones include House of Highlights and MLG Highlights, and Chris Smoove.

Watching old NBA clips

Then, I began binge-watching many complications that YouTubers began to make relating to impressive plays, lowlights, etc. I watched them a few hours a day, and I found them interesting because I was seeing basketball plays that I had never saw before. Channels such as NiTringo NBA would make videos such as NBA "Low" Arcing Shots and NBA "ARE YOU BLIND?!" Moments, and they always impressed me or made me laugh. However, after a while, I got extremely bored with these type of videos since I was watching the same NBA moments over and over again. For example, a half-court buzzer to win the game can be considered as a "WOW Moment" and a "game winner."

Watching NBA YouTubers to learn more about the NBA

During the summers of 2017, 2018 and 2019, I spent five to six hours a day watching YouTube vidoes to learn about NBA history and current events (eg. (historical) trades, GOAT candidates, NBA matchups, NBA what ifs, NBA stories, trash talks, NBA records, players' stats, etc). I watched YouTubers such as Mike Korzemba, Andy Hoops, MDJ, JxmyHighroller, Dom2k, HandlesUP, and countless other channels.

Doing NBA Sporcle quizzes

I started doing NBA Sporcle quizzes because the NBA YouTubers I was watching inspired me to do so. Many of them were getting good scores, and I also wanted to get similar scores to them. As a result, I doing quizzes such as NBA TOP 25 BY CATEGORY, ESPN'S 100 GREATEST NBA PLAYERS OF ALL-TIME, and NBA MVPs. At first, I was getting bad scores, as I didn't know much about the NBA. However, through repetition and doing them over and over again, I started getting decent scores and memorizing random facts.

Looking at the News to keep up with the current events

Nowadays, because of school and a decline in interest (the more important factor), I check the news once in a while to keep up with trades and standings. Unlike before, I don't search up the game results everyday because I feel that things would be more interesting to me if I checked everything at one time.




Opinions

Compared to last season, I think the officiating has gotten a bit better. For the past few years, the NBA has been getting softer because of how many players have found loopholes around the rulebook. More flops have been allowed, and although the NBA made a rule that stated that they would fine players who flopped, the rule was a joke, as it was never really enforced. Although there have been some improvments, I still think that there is a long way to go before the officiating can get good again. For example, nowadays, refs hand out technical fouls for the simplest things. Like how Cade Cunningham got ejected for pointing at his teammate as a celebration after he postered someone. Or how

Personally, I don't understand why the Lakers' bench players (such as Kuzma and Caldwell-Pope) were trashed so much. I understand that they're playing fro LeBron, who always carries high expectations everywhere he goes, but stil... They literally traded ... for Westbrook when they could have gotten Buddy Hield, who would have been a much better fit with LeBron and Anthony Davis. Westbrook's skillsket is kind of redundant because LeBron is a better playmaker and slasher than him, and he can't shoot, so.. when he doesn't have the ball, he's not much use since the opponent can double team others and leave him wide open.

Many people say that Stephen Curry is just a shooter, and I do not agree with this opinion. I understand that his shooting is what he is most known for, but he impacts the game in so many ways other than shooting long-distance shots. For example, his off-ball movement is insane. He's always running around, settings screens, etc, and that opens up many opportunities for his teammates. Also, he's one of the superstars who actually puts a lot effort into defending (despite his physical limitations), and though he was always picked on in the playoffs (especially in the Western Conference Finals in 2018 against the Rockets), I would say that he's an average defender since he would obviously be considered the weak link playing alongside Klay Thompson, Andre Iguodola, Kevin Durant, and Draymond Green. He's very good in the passing lanes, and that showed when he led the league in steals during his historic season in 2016.

Out of all the trades in NBA history, I think that the most impactful one is the when Ed McCauley and Cliff Hagan were traded from the Boston Celtics to the St. Louis Hawks from the second pick of the 1956 NBA Draft (which turned out to be Bill Russell). The most obvious reason is because how Russell was the anchor of a team that went on to will 11 championships in 13 years. There have been many trades for superstar players, but I think this one is the impactful because of how early in NBA history this happened. It's been almost 70 years, as we know with the Butterfly effect, one minor tweak in history can cause a chain of events to occur. If the Celtics didn't have Russell on their team, maybe they would have selected higher in the draft (since they wouldn't have been so good). Or they make trades to acquire players. Also, maybe the St. Louis Hawks wouldn't have located to Atlanta in the late 1960s if Russell was on the team. Attendance hadn't been good throughout the 1960s, but that could have changed if they were winning more. This, in turn, would have led to other events that. I could go on and on and on, but that's all I'll say for now.

NBA Video Games
